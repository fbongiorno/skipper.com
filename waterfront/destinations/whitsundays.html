Australia's Sailing Paradise

74 Islands, One
Perfect Passage

The Whitsundays sit in the heart of the Great Barrier Reef Marine Park, 900 kilometres north of Brisbane. Seventy-four continental islands rise from the Coral Sea, most of them uninhabited national park, fringed by coral reefs and separated by passages of crystal-clear water. The trade winds blow reliably from the southeast from April to November, making the Whitsundays one of the world's most accessible and rewarding sailing destinations.

The centrepiece is Whitehaven Beach — 7 kilometres of pure silica sand so fine it squeaks underfoot and so white it doesn't absorb heat even in 35°C sunshine. Hill Inlet's swirling sandbars of brilliant white against turquoise water create aerial photos that seem impossible. All of this accessible only to those who arrive by boat.

The view from the Hill Inlet lookout — swirling white sand and turquoise water, accessible only via Whitehaven Beach

"The Whitsundays offer a rare gift: world-class sailing in pristine national park waters, with Whitehaven Beach — arguably the finest beach on earth — as the prize at the end of every passage."

Top Destinations

Six Unmissable Stops

01
World's Best Beach

Whitehaven Beach

Seven kilometres of pure 98% silica sand — the whitest, finest beach on earth. Dinghy ashore at the southern or northern end and walk the entire length. The sand is cool to the touch even in full summer sun. Absolutely no facilities, no development, no footprints.

National park beach · Anchor offshore in 4–8m · Dinghy ashore at S end · Sunset here is unforgettable
02
Aerial Wonder

Hill Inlet

At the northern end of Whitehaven Beach, Hill Inlet is a tidal inlet where sandbars of brilliant white swirl against every shade of turquoise and blue. The lookout (20 min walk from the beach) delivers the most iconic view in the Whitsundays.

20min walk north on Whitehaven · Best at high tide for colour contrast · Sunrise most spectacular
03
Premier Snorkelling

Blue Pearl Bay

On Hayman Island, Blue Pearl Bay is considered the finest snorkelling site in the Whitsundays. Bommies of coral rise from the sandy seafloor and are alive with fish, turtles and occasionally reef sharks. Protected from the trade winds by the island's hills.

Anchor in 6–10m · Coral bommies off N shore · Turtles common · Mooring buoys available
04
Protected Anchorage

Nara Inlet

A dramatic fjord-like inlet on Hook Island, with near-vertical cliffs rising directly from the water's edge. One of the most protected anchorages in the Whitsundays — ideal for sitting out bad weather or simply enjoying the extraordinary silence and birdsong.

Deep, protected anchorage · Aboriginal rock art site · Complete shelter from trades
05
Hidden Gem

Langford Island Spit

At low tide, a spectacular sandbar extends from Langford Island — shallow enough to stand in, fringed by coral. The water changes from white to aqua to deep blue in the space of twenty metres. Anchor on the sand spit side and wade ashore.

Tidal sandbar at low water · Snorkel the fringing reef · Anchor in 3–5m on sand only
06
Great Barrier Reef

Hardy Reef

Sail 40nm offshore to the outer Great Barrier Reef at Hardy Reef. The reef wall drops from the surface to 40 metres and is alive with sharks, rays, giant grouper and thousands of tropical fish. The Heart Reef — a naturally heart-shaped coral formation — is nearby.

40nm offshore — weather-permitting only · Permit required · Heart Reef nearby (aerial view)

Essential Knowledge

The Sailor's Whitsundays Guide

Everything you need to know before you cast off the dock lines at Abel Point Marina, Airlie Beach.

Navigation & Tidal Awareness

The Whitsundays sit in a semi-enclosed sea where tidal ranges reach 4 metres at spring tides. This makes for strong tidal currents in the passages between islands — the Whitsunday Passage carries 2–4 knots at peak flow. Plan passages around the tide.

  • Use Australian Hydrographic charts (AUS chart 820, 822, 823)
  • Tidal range: up to 4m at springs — check daily tables
  • Whitsunday Passage: main north–south route, strong tidal current
  • Shoal areas mark with waypoints — especially near Hayman
  • No anchoring on coral — GBRMPA rules strictly enforced (high fines)
  • Monitor VHF Ch 16; work channels 72 and 67

Seasons & Cyclone Awareness

The Whitsundays have a clearly defined sailing season. April to November brings reliable ESE trade winds of 15–25 knots and fine settled weather. The Australian summer (December to March) is cyclone season — no chartering recommended during this period.

  • Best sailing: May–October (reliable ESE trades, clear skies)
  • Shoulder: April and November (variable conditions, fewer boats)
  • Cyclone season: December–March (avoid entirely)
  • Water temp: 22°C (winter) to 28°C (summer)
  • Air temp: 22–30°C during sailing season
  • Check BOM weather: bom.gov.au daily

Great Barrier Reef Regulations

The Whitsundays sit within the Great Barrier Reef Marine Park — one of the world's most strictly protected marine environments. All visitors must comply with GBRMPA regulations. Fines for violations are severe.

  • Environmental Management Charge (EMC) payable per person per night
  • No anchoring on coral — sand anchoring only
  • Fishing: zoning applies — check your area carefully
  • No take of any marine organism from green zones
  • Permitted anchoring areas shown on official GBRMPA charts
  • Onboard waste: no discharge within the Marine Park (use pump-out at marina)

Airlie Beach & Provisioning

Airlie Beach is the gateway to the Whitsundays and offers everything a charter skipper needs. Abel Point Marina has full services, a large Coles supermarket is a 10-minute walk away, and the town has excellent restaurants and equipment hire.

  • Abel Point Marina: full services, fuel dock, chandlery
  • Coles Airlie Beach: 10min walk, full provisioning
  • Dive shops: multiple operators for equipment and gas
  • Ice: available at marina and fuel dock
  • Hire gear: snorkelling, kayaks, paddleboards all available in town
  • Emergency haul-out: Hamilton Island Marina (full travel lift)

At a Glance

Practical Information

CountryAustralia. State of Queensland. No visa required for citizens of USA, UK, EU, NZ, Canada (ETA required for some). Strongly English-speaking.
CurrencyAustralian Dollar (AUD). ATMs at Airlie Beach and Hamilton Island. Cards accepted everywhere. Cash for national park fees.
LanguageEnglish. No language barrier.
Charter BasesAbel Point Marina, Airlie Beach. Hamilton Island Marina (accessible by ferry from Airlie). Main operators: Sunsail, Moorings, Queensland Yacht Charters, Cumberland Charter Yachts.
Best SeasonMay–October for consistent ESE trade winds, clear skies and calm seas. July–August peak season — book early. April and November quieter with similar conditions.
Emergency ContactsAustralian Maritime Safety Authority (AMSA): VHF Ch 16. AMSA 24-hour: +61 2 6230 6811. QLD Marine Rescue: VHF Ch 73. Emergency: 000 (Australia).
Time ZoneAustralian Eastern Standard Time (AEST) — UTC+10. Queensland does not observe daylight saving.
Marine Park FeesEnvironmental Management Charge (EMC) ~AUD$7.50 per person per night payable on departure. GBRMPA zoning maps available at reef.gbrmpa.gov.au.
Must Do AshoreHill Inlet lookout walk (free, 20min), Airlie Beach Lagoon (free public pool), Conway National Park hiking, Prosail tall ship day charter, seaplane to Heart Reef.
